# Thinker Cards

Use these as decision lenses. They are not literal simulations and should not be treated as endorsements.

## Elon Musk

Role: the first-principles engineer.

Use when the decision involves cost, manufacturing, speed, technical feasibility, process design, or a bloated system.

Core models:

- Asymptotic limit: calculate the theoretical minimum cost, time, or physical limit before accepting industry assumptions.
- Idiot index: compare finished price with raw material or fundamental input cost.
- Five-step algorithm: question requirements, delete, simplify, accelerate, then automate.
- Vertical integration: control the costly bottleneck when the middle layers create waste.
- Fast iteration: learn from reversible failures faster than planning can predict.

Questions:

- What is the physical or economic limit?
- Which requirement should not exist?
- Who requested this requirement by name?
- Is the gap caused by physics or by convention?
- Can failure be recovered from? If yes, why are you not testing faster?

Boundary:

Strong for engineering, manufacturing, cost structure, and aggressive iteration. Weak for empathy-heavy problems, institutional knowledge, public trust, and situations where failure is irreversible.

## Charlie Munger

Role: the error-avoidance operator.

Use when the decision can fail because of bad incentives, obvious stupidity, hidden fatal flaws, or overconfidence.

Core models:

- Inversion: ask how to guarantee failure, then avoid those paths.
- Circle of competence: sort decisions into yes, no, and too hard.
- Incentives: show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome.
- Lollapalooza effect: multiple biases can reinforce each other into extreme error.
- Latticework: use multiple models, not one hammer.

Questions:

- How can this destroy you?
- Is this inside your real circle of competence?
- Can you explain the strongest opposing case better than your opponent can?
- Who makes money here, and who bears the risk?
- Is there one fatal flaw that makes all the strengths irrelevant?

Boundary:

Strong for avoiding stupidity, reading incentives, and protecting long-term compounding. Weak when speed, creative exploration, or asymmetric upside requires temporary mess.

## Nassim Taleb

Role: the ruin-risk detector.

Use when downside, fragility, leverage, expert advice, or irreversible loss matters.

Core models:

- Asymmetric risk: do not average away a downside that can wipe you out.
- Antifragility: prefer positions that benefit from volatility.
- Skin in the game: discount advice from people who do not bear consequences.
- Lindy effect: things that have survived time deserve extra respect.
- Via negativa: remove harm before adding solutions.

Questions:

- What is the worst case, and can you survive it?
- If you repeated this strategy many times, would one event wipe you out?
- Who is giving advice without paying the price if wrong?
- Are you fragile, robust, or antifragile here?
- What should be removed before anything is added?

Boundary:

Strong for survival, leverage, tail risk, and expert skepticism. Weak for detailed execution, incremental operational choices, and situations where ordinary expected-value thinking is enough.

## Naval Ravikant

Role: the leverage and desire auditor.

Use when the decision involves career direction, freedom, wealth, identity, long-term games, or choosing what not to want.

Core models:

- Leverage: labor, capital, code, and media multiply judgment.
- Specific knowledge: the best work feels like play to you and work to others.
- Desire as contract: every desire is a contract to be unhappy until fulfilled.
- Redefinition: change the meaning of the key word and the decision may change.
- Long-term games: play repeated games with long-term people.

Questions:

- Is output tied linearly to your time or multiplied by leverage?
- Which form of leverage does this use?
- Is this your specific knowledge or someone else's game?
- Which desires are fighting each other?
- What does freedom, success, or wealth mean in this decision?

Boundary:

Strong for leverage, independence, and long-term direction. Weak when immediate survival, mental health, or detailed operating plans matter more than principles.

## Richard Feynman

Role: the anti-self-deception scientist.

Use when you may be using names, jargon, rituals, or official stories instead of understanding.

Core models:

- Naming is not understanding.
- The first principle is not to fool yourself.
- Uncertainty is strength, not weakness.
- Concrete demonstration beats verbal explanation.
- Curiosity reveals hidden structure.

Questions:

- Can you explain this without jargon to a smart child?
- Do you believe it because of evidence or because you want it to be true?
- What would a smart critic use against you?
- Can you demonstrate it in ten seconds?
- Did you verify this yourself or only hear a report?

Boundary:

Strong for clarity, verification, and exposing cargo-cult thinking. Weak for diplomacy, emotional management, and social situations that require tact before truth.

## Steve Jobs

Role: the focus and end-to-end experience judge.

Use when product taste, simplicity, user experience, focus, or life direction matters.

Core models:

- Focus means saying no to many good ideas.
- End-to-end control matters when the experience depends on every link.
- Start from user experience, not available technology.
- Users can describe pain but not always imagine the future.
- Death clarifies what matters.
- Technology must meet the humanities.

Questions:

- Can you define this in one sentence without technical parameters?
- Which 99 good ideas are you willing to cut?
- Who controls the key part of the final experience?
- What does the user feel the first time they use it?
- Are the hidden parts built with the same care as the visible parts?
- If time were short, would this still matter?

Boundary:

Strong for product focus, taste, integration, and experience. Weak if copied as management style, if used as an excuse for arrogance, or if applied outside the domain where you have deep user understanding.

## Andrej Karpathy

Role: the engineering realist.

Use when AI, software, demos, deployment, reliability, learning, or technical understanding matters.

Core models:

- March of nines: going from demo to reliability is harder than going from zero to demo.
- Building is understanding: if you cannot rebuild the core, you may not understand it.
- LLMs are dream machines: hallucination is not a small bug but part of the mechanism.
- Jagged intelligence: models are brilliant in some places and bizarrely weak in others.
- Suit of armor, not robot: great AI products amplify humans before removing them.
- Software's third paradigm: natural language is becoming a programming interface.

Questions:

- What happens in the hardest five percent of cases?
- What happens at the millionth or billionth use?
- Can you rebuild the core from scratch?
- Is this a suit of armor for humans or a robot replacing them?
- Where are the jagged failure points?
- Which parts are verified and which are guessed?

Boundary:

Strong for AI product judgment, reliability, and human-AI workflow design. Weak for marketing, fundraising, politics, and problems where being too engineering-realistic kills a valid creative bet.

## Paul Graham

Role: the clarity, startup, and early-user lens.

Use when the decision involves writing, startups, product direction, early users, taste, or unconventional opportunities.

Core models:

- Writing is thinking: if you cannot write it clearly, you do not understand it.
- Taste is trained judgment.
- Build first, discover truth through use.
- Superlinear returns: some paths compound far beyond linear effort.
- Independent thinking: most people are repeating local consensus.

Questions:

- Write the decision as one paragraph. Where does it get vague?
- Are you solving a real problem you have seen, or guessing from a distance?
- Do a few people love it, or do many people mildly approve?
- Are you default alive or default dead?
- What view do you hold that your peers would find uncomfortable?
- Are you avoiding the dirty work that makes the opportunity real?

Boundary:

Strong for early-stage products, writing, user love, and startup judgment. Weak outside the startup and technology context, especially where social structure, regulation, or lack of safety net dominates.

## Ilya Sutskever

Role: the deep-understanding and research-taste lens.

Use when the decision involves AI, research direction, scale, safety, understanding, or whether a path has reached a phase boundary.

Core models:

- Compression is understanding: prediction works when the underlying structure is captured.
- Improvement is not phase change: more resources can improve a path without transforming it.
- Safety and capability are linked: control requires understanding.
- Super learner, not database: learning speed matters more than stored knowledge.
- Silence as information architecture: what you do not say is part of the strategy.
- Research taste: elegant, simple directions are more trustworthy than patch piles.

Questions:

- Is this understanding or memorization?
- Does it transfer to unseen cases?
- Are you buying improvement or real phase change?
- Is the bottleneck resources or ideas?
- Is the solution elegant, or is it surviving on patches?
- What should not be said yet?

Boundary:

Strong for AI direction, research taste, and distinguishing real capability from surface performance. Weak for organizational execution, politics, human incentives, and concrete go-to-market planning.

## Donald Trump

Role: the negotiation and narrative lens.

Use when the decision is a deal, public conflict, pricing, leverage game, media story, or power contest.

Core models:

- Everything is a deal: map the chips, incentives, and alternatives.
- Extreme anchor: start high to create room.
- Unpredictability can be leverage.
- Victim narrative can mobilize a base.
- Redefine victory after failure, but do not let public narrative corrupt private learning.
- Audience reaction matters in public communication.

Questions:

- Is this actually a negotiation?
- What do you have that the other side wants?
- What is your opening anchor?
- Who needs the deal more?
- What is your Plan B?
- How will your audience interpret the story?
- Are you using narrative externally while still being honest internally?

Boundary:

Strong for negotiation, anchoring, leverage, public narrative, and conflict. Weak for truth-seeking, long-term trust, cooperative relationships, and expert-heavy domains where facts cannot be negotiated away.

## MrBeast

Role: the attention and retention operator.

Use when the decision depends on being noticed, understood, shared, retained, or scaled through audience response.

Core models:

- Click and stay: attention is acquisition multiplied by retention.
- Communication can matter more than the raw idea.
- Simple concept, extreme execution.
- Reinvestment flywheel.
- Constraint-driven creativity.
- Ten-year horizon and problem-solver identity.

Questions:

- Is the problem that people do not click, or that they do not stay?
- Can the concept excite a stranger in one sentence?
- Are you spending enough effort on packaging and transmission?
- What can be reinvested to make the next version better?
- Is the constraint actually forcing a more creative solution?
- Are you judging this on a quarter or a decade?

Boundary:

Strong for packaging, attention, retention, content, and audience growth. Weak when human cost, ethics, legal risk, or platform dependence must dominate the decision.
