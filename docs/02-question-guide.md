# Question Guide

The quality of the roundtable depends on the quality of your question.

Do not ask:

> Should I quit my job?

Ask with context:

- What is happening?
- What are the real options?
- What are you afraid of?
- What constraint cannot be ignored?
- What do you want from the roundtable?

## The Three-Layer Format

### 1. Situation

State facts before feelings.

Include:

- Current position
- Options you are considering
- Time window
- Money or resource limits
- Reversibility
- Who else is affected

### 2. Fear

Say the uncomfortable part.

Include:

- What could go wrong
- What outcome would be unacceptable
- What you may be avoiding
- What assumption you secretly hope is true

### 3. Desired Output

Tell the system what you need.

Examples:

- "I need to see the blind spots."
- "I need a first test, not a final answer."
- "I need to know whether the downside can kill me."
- "I need to know whether this is real leverage or just busy work."

## Optional: Pick Extra Lenses

The default four are always present:

- Musk
- Munger
- Taleb
- Naval

Add optional lenses when needed:

- Feynman: if you may not understand the thing clearly.
- Jobs: if focus, taste, or experience matters.
- Karpathy: if AI, engineering reliability, or deployment risk matters.
- Paul Graham: if early users, writing, startups, or product direction matter.
- Ilya: if understanding, scale, safety, or research direction matters.
- Trump: if negotiation, leverage, or public narrative matters.
- MrBeast: if attention, packaging, or retention matters.

## Template A: Career or Startup Decision

```text
I want to run a Thinkers Roundtable on one decision.

Situation:
I am [age/context]. I currently [job/business/project]. I am considering [option A] versus [option B]. I have [money/time/runway]. The decision has [deadline/reversibility/risk].

Fear:
The worst case is [specific downside]. I may be fooling myself about [assumption]. I may also be avoiding [uncomfortable truth].

Desired output:
I do not want motivation. I want to know whether my core assumption is real, what the first test should be, and what would prove this decision wrong.

Please especially bring in [optional lenses].
```

## Template B: Resource Allocation Decision

```text
I want to run a Thinkers Roundtable on one decision.

Situation:
I am splitting my time between [project 1], [project 2], and [project 3]. I have only [available time/energy]. None of them is compounding clearly yet.

Fear:
I may be calling this a time-management problem when it is really a refusal to choose. I am afraid of choosing wrong, so I keep everything alive.

Desired output:
Force me to identify what to cut, what to test, and which assumption should decide the priority.

Please especially bring in Munger, Jobs, Naval, and Paul Graham.
```

## Template C: AI or Product Decision

```text
I want to run a Thinkers Roundtable on one decision.

Situation:
I am building [product/workflow/system]. The demo works, but I am unsure whether it is reliable enough for real use. The biggest uncertainty is [technical/user/business risk].

Fear:
I may be confusing a convincing demo with a deployable system. I may also be hiding behind technical polish instead of testing whether people actually want it.

Desired output:
Tell me what to test first, which failure mode matters most, and whether this should be a suit of armor for users or a fully autonomous robot.

Please especially bring in Karpathy, Feynman, Jobs, and MrBeast.
```
